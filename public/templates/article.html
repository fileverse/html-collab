The quiet comeback of local-first software

AWBy A. Writer · June 9, 2026 · 6 min read

For a decade the cloud swallowed everything. Now a counter-current is gaining strength: software that works on your device first and syncs second.

— cover image —

Why it matters

Local-first apps stay fast and usable offline, keep data under the user's control, and still collaborate when a network is available. For years that combination felt impossible — you picked speed or sync, ownership or collaboration. New tools make you pick less.

  • Instant interactions — no round-trip to a server for every keystroke.
  • Ownership — your data lives with you, not only on someone else's machine.
  • Resilience — the app keeps working when the network doesn't.
The network is an enhancement, not a dependency.
— the local-first manifesto, paraphrased

The trade-offs

Conflict resolution and sync are genuinely hard. Two people editing the same paragraph offline will, eventually, disagree. But modern CRDTs have made these conflicts tractable for everyday apps, resolving most of them automatically and surfacing the rest in ways humans can reason about.

The cost shows up elsewhere: larger client bundles, trickier debugging, and a smaller ecosystem of libraries. Teams adopting local-first trade operational simplicity on the server for complexity on the client.

What's ahead

Expect the boundary to keep blurring. The best products won't advertise "local-first" at all — users will simply notice that things feel instant, keep working on a plane, and never lose a draft. That quiet reliability is the whole point.
